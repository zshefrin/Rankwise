How to Choose an HVAC Marketing Agency in Metro Vancouver

When choosing an HVAC marketing agency in Metro Vancouver, three criteria matter more than the rest: (1) the agency works with only one HVAC contractor per city, eliminating internal conflicts of interest; (2) monthly fees are tied to measurable outcomes like call volume and ranking positions, not just deliverables like posts published or reports sent; and (3) the agreement runs month-to-month with 30 days notice to cancel, so leaving is possible if results don't show up. The rest of this article covers BC-specific market context, what to expect each month, and the exact questions to ask before signing anything.

Choosing an HVAC marketing agency in Metro Vancouver comes down to four filters: does the agency know the BC market specifically, do they offer exclusivity in your city, are their fees tied to measurable outcomes rather than activity, and can you leave without penalty if results don't show up? An agency that clears all four is worth a conversation. One that clears none will feel familiar — keep looking.

Whitespark's annual Local Search Ranking Factors survey consistently ranks Google review signals — count, average rating, recency — among the top three factors driving Map Pack rankings, alongside proximity and primary GBP category. In a competitive HVAC market like Metro Vancouver, that means a 4.5+ average rating with a healthy, recent review count isn't a stretch goal — it's the competitive floor a good agency should be calibrating your targets against before they take your money, not after.

Does the agency understand the Metro Vancouver market specifically?

BC has its own search patterns. CleanBC heat pump rebates — up to $6,000 under current program tiers — generate real search volume in the Lower Mainland that US-market benchmarks won't capture. Homeowner search behaviour in Burnaby differs from Surrey. The competitive landscape in Coquitlam is not the same as the competitive landscape in Langley.

An agency optimizing for HVAC search traffic using American directory relationships, US seasonal baselines, and generic "home services" keyword lists is solving a different problem. Ask them directly: what's your benchmark review count for a top-3 Map Pack position in my city? If they reach for a US industry average, they're not working from BC data.

Do they offer exclusivity — one contractor per city?

Most agencies work with multiple contractors in the same market. That's a structural conflict. An agency earning monthly fees from three HVAC contractors in Burnaby has no particular incentive to push hard on any one of them — they're already earning from all three.

Exclusivity means one contractor per city, enforced. Not a preference. Not "we try to avoid conflicts." A hard rule with a clear definition of what "city" means — the municipality, not a service area radius that conveniently avoids the issue.

Before signing anything, ask: do you currently work with any other HVAC contractor in my city? If yes, that's a disqualifier — walk away.

Are fees tied to outcomes or activity?

Most agency retainers are structured around deliverables: posts published, audits completed, reports sent. These are activity metrics. The contractor pays the same whether call volume goes up, stays flat, or drops.

The alternative is a performance model: specific monthly targets — search appearances, ranking positions, calls from Google — agreed in writing before work starts. Miss the target, no payment for that month.

Ask every agency you're evaluating: what specific targets will you be accountable to each month? What happens if you miss them? A vague answer ("we'll work toward improving your visibility") is an activity model dressed up in outcome language. A clear answer names numbers and a consequence.

Can you leave without a long-term contract?

The standard agency contract in this space runs 6 to 12 months. That means you're locked in through a full SEO cycle regardless of whether results appear. A month-to-month model with 30 days notice to cancel inverts this — the agency stays accountable for results every month, not just at the end of a contract term.

Lock-in clauses exist because agencies need time to show results. That's a real argument — SEO takes 60 to 90 days to show ranking movement and 6 months to compound. But a performance guarantee covers the same concern: if the targets aren't being hit, you're not paying anyway.

What should you expect from an HVAC marketing agency each month?

A legitimate agency should deliver, at minimum:

1. Weekly Google Business Profile management — posts, photo uploads, review responses, category and attribute accuracy 2. On-page local SEO — service-and-city page optimization for queries like "heat pump installation Burnaby" or "furnace repair Surrey" 3. Citation management — consistent business information across directories including Yelp, HomeStars, BBB, and Yellow Pages Canada 4. Monthly rank reporting — where you appear in Maps and organic search for your target queries, compared to the prior month 5. Content publishing — blog posts targeting the questions your homeowners are actually searching

If an agency isn't delivering all five, ask which they're skipping and why. An agency that manages your GBP but ignores your citation footprint is leaving a known ranking signal unaddressed.

According to BrightLocal's 2024 Local Consumer Review Survey, 87% of consumers read online reviews for local businesses before making a decision. For HVAC contractors, that means review response cadence — how quickly and how well you respond — is a direct input to conversion, not just reputation management. It should be in scope.

What questions should you ask before hiring?

Before committing to any HVAC marketing agency in Metro Vancouver, get clear answers to these:

  • Do you work with any other HVAC contractor in my city right now? Exclusivity is either a hard rule or it isn't.
  • What specific monthly targets will you be held to? Get numbers, not categories.
  • What happens if you miss a target? If the answer isn't "you don't pay," it's an activity model.
  • What's the contract term? Month-to-month vs. 6–12 month lock-in is a meaningful difference in risk.
  • How do you set targets — from BC market data or industry benchmarks? The answer tells you whether they've done homework on your actual competitive landscape.
  • What does the monthly deliverable list look like? GBP, citations, content, rank reporting — each one should be named specifically.

A contractor who's been burned by an agency before will recognize the difference between answers that are specific and answers that sound specific. "We optimize your online presence" is not an answer. "We publish two GBP posts per week, respond to reviews within 24 hours, and send you a rank report on the first of every month for these 12 keyword targets" is.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does HVAC marketing cost in Metro Vancouver? Local marketing retainers for HVAC contractors in Metro Vancouver typically run between $500 and $2,500 per month depending on scope and city competition level. Paid media — Google Ads, Meta — is separate and typically quoted per-project. An agency that gives you a number before reviewing your market isn't doing the math correctly.

How long does it take to see results from HVAC marketing in BC? Google Business Profile improvements typically show within 30 days — more profile views, more clicks. Ranking movement on target search terms takes 60 to 90 days. Consistent, predictable inbound call volume compounds over 6 months or more. Any agency promising top-3 rankings by next month is not giving you an honest timeline.

Should I hire a local Metro Vancouver agency or a national one? For HVAC search in Metro Vancouver, local expertise matters more than scale. A national or US-based agency will apply benchmarks from a different market — different review counts, different directory ecosystems, different seasonal patterns. BC-specific context — CleanBC rebate search volume, Lower Mainland neighbourhood-level competition, Canadian directory coverage — is not something a US-market agency has built into their defaults.

What's the difference between a performance guarantee and a standard retainer? A standard retainer ties fees to deliverables — posts published, reports sent. A performance guarantee ties fees to outcomes — specific ranking positions, search appearances, call volume. If the outcome targets aren't met, you don't pay for that month. These are structurally different accountability models, and the difference becomes obvious the first month results don't appear.

Is exclusivity — one contractor per city — actually enforced, or is it a marketing claim? Ask the agency directly whether they currently have any HVAC contractor clients in your city. A genuine exclusivity policy has a clear answer. "We try to avoid conflicts" is not a policy.
