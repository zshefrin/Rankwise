How Many Reviews Do HVAC Contractors Need to Rank Locally?

There's no fixed number — what matters is whether you're posting reviews more recently than competitors ranking above you. In many Metro Vancouver markets, the threshold is much lower than contractors assume: top-ranked contractors in Surrey and Vancouver hold Map Pack positions with fewer than 60 reviews total.

According to BrightLocal's 2024 Local Consumer Review Survey, 93% of consumers say online reviews influence their purchasing decisions. But Google's ranking algorithm cares more about review patterns than raw totals.

Does More Always Mean Better?

Not for rankings. A contractor with 400 reviews and no new ones in six months will often rank below a competitor with 45 reviews who got 8 in the last 90 days. Google's ranking signal from reviews has three components that matter more than the count:

  • Velocity — how frequently new reviews are arriving
  • Recency — how recently the latest reviews were posted
  • Response rate — whether the business owner is acknowledging reviews, positive and negative

A profile that looks actively maintained ranks better than one that looks abandoned, regardless of historical totals.

What the Metro Vancouver Map Pack Actually Shows

According to Rankwise's April 2026 SERP analysis across five Metro Vancouver cities, the review spread between top-ranked contractors is wide. In Surrey, contractors ranking in the top 3 for "HVAC contractor Surrey" had between 48 and 1,500 reviews — a 30× gap between the highest and lowest. In North Vancouver, the spread for the same search was 171 to 2,800 reviews. In Vancouver itself, some top-ranked contractors held positions with under 80 reviews, sitting beside long-established businesses with ten times as many.

The takeaway: you don't need to match the biggest player in your city. You need enough reviews, posted recently enough, that Google treats your profile as actively maintained — and enough that a homeowner landing on your profile doesn't hesitate before calling.

How Many Is "Enough"?

A rough benchmark based on what's working in Metro Vancouver right now:

  • Smaller cities (Langley, Delta, Maple Ridge): 25–40 reviews, at least 5 in the last 90 days
  • Mid-tier cities (Burnaby, North Vancouver, Richmond): 50–80 reviews, 8–12 in the last quarter
  • Vancouver proper: 75–100 reviews, consistent velocity month-over-month

These aren't guarantees. Category setup, proximity, GBP activity, and site authority all factor in. But if your review count is below these ranges, it's worth addressing before touching other ranking variables.

Why Review Recency Matters More Than You Think

Homeowners reading your profile do the same mental calculation Google does: old reviews suggest the business may have changed, for better or worse. BrightLocal's 2024 Local Consumer Review Survey found that 85% of consumers consider reviews older than three months less relevant.

A contractor who got 80 reviews in 2021 and has had silence since then faces a real credibility problem — not just a ranking one. New homeowners can't know if that business still operates the same way, serves their neighbourhood, or takes on the type of job they need.

One new genuine review every two to three weeks is enough to keep the velocity signal healthy without looking like you're artificially pushing volume.

How to Ask for Reviews and Actually Get Them

The most reliable method is a direct ask right after the job, while the homeowner is still in front of you or on the phone for a post-job check-in. Most contractors never ask — and that alone explains the review gap between contractors with similar service quality.

What works:

  • Ask verbally at the end of the job: "If everything went well today, would you mind leaving us a quick Google review? It really helps."
  • Text a direct link to your Google review form within an hour of completing the job — while the homeowner is still thinking about you
  • Respond to every review you receive, including the negative ones. Homeowners reading your profile notice when the owner engages

What doesn't work: sending generic automated emails weeks later, asking friends and family who didn't use the service, or buying reviews. Google detects review manipulation patterns and suppresses profiles that trigger them.

The Bigger Picture

Reviews are one piece of Map Pack rankings, not the whole picture. A contractor with 100 reviews and a wrong primary category will still rank below a competitor with 40 reviews and the right setup. Getting the profile fundamentals right first — then building review velocity — is the sequence that produces results.
